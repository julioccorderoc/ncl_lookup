The Silence After "How Are You?": Why The Non-Response Happens in Chronic Illness

Short Answer

When friends or family stop replying immediately after you provide an honest health update, it is rarely an act of malice. It is often a phenomenon known as "discomfort paralysis." The listener, lacking the tools to fix your problem or process the reality of chronic suffering, shuts down emotionally. Navigating this requires adjusting expectations, protecting your energy, and understanding that their silence is a reflection of their capacity, not your worth.

"More than half of the time, I tell someone how I'm doing, and they don't text me back. For context, they asked--I don't talk about my health unprompted... 'I'm OK. Just kind of the same. In bed all of the time. Reading a lot, though.' No response. Or they respond to the reading part but ignore the health part... It is so hurtful. It's humiliating to feel vulnerable, sharing anything about my health, only to be met with rejection."

This experience, described vividly in a recent online discussion, highlights one of the most painful secondary symptoms of chronic illness: the "Non-response." It is a specific type of social rejection where vulnerability is met with silence. For those living with conditions like ME/CFS, Long Covid, or Lyme disease, this silence can feel like a confirmation that their reality is too heavy for others to hold.

Understanding why this happens—and realizing it is a systemic social issue rather than a personal failure—is the first step toward protecting your peace.

Why do friends go silent after asking how I am?

The dynamic is confusing because the friend initiated the contact. They asked, "How are you?" Yet, when the answer is anything other than "Fine," communication halts. Psychologically, this is often caused by a misalignment of social scripts.

In general society, "How are you?" is often a phatic expression—a social pleasantry meant to signal acknowledgement, not an inquiry into wellbeing. When a person with a chronic illness answers the question literally (e.g., "I am severe and bedbound"), it breaks the social script. The listener may experience:

  • The Fixer’s Dilemma: Many people are conditioned to believe that if a problem is stated, they must offer a solution. When faced with a complex chronic illness that cannot be fixed, they feel helpless. To avoid that feeling of helplessness, they disengage.
  • Mortality Mirroring: Hearing about intractable illness reminds healthy people of their own fragility. This unconscious fear can trigger an avoidance response.
  • Emotional Bandwidth: As noted in community discussions, some individuals simply lack the emotional depth or stamina to sit with discomfort. They are not necessarily malicious; they are emotionally unskilled.

Is it wrong to be honest about my health?

A common internal reaction to the non-response is shame. You may wonder, "Did I complain too much?" or "Am I being too negative?"

It is vital to distinguish between complaining and reporting.

  • Complaining often implies a desire for pity or a refusal to accept a situation.
  • Reporting is a factual update on your current reality.

If you are bedbound, stating "I am in bed all the time" is a neutral fact, similar to someone saying, "I am at the office all day." You are not answering "wrong." You are existing in a reality that our culture has not taught people how to acknowledge gracefully. The humiliation you feel is valid, but it belongs to the situation, not to you.

How does social stress impact physical health?

Navigating these emotional minefields is not just a mental burden; it is a physical one. For those with energy-limiting conditions, the stress of rejection can trigger Post-Exertional Malaise (PEM).

Processing rejection releases cortisol and requires significant cognitive energy—resources that are often scarce. Protecting your emotional environment is a key part of pacing. Just as you might support your body physically—perhaps by resting or exploring researched supplements like monolaurin and lysine for immune resilience—you must also support your body by curating a social environment that minimizes stress triggers.

What are practical ways to handle the conversation?

While you cannot control how others respond, you can alter how you engage to minimize your own hurt. Here are three strategies often used by the chronic illness community:

  • The "Off-Ramp" Technique: When answering, include a question back to them immediately.
  • Example:* "Health is tough this week, mostly resting. But I finally started that new show. How are things going with your job?"
  • Why it works:* It gives the listener permission to move the conversation forward without feeling stuck in the "heavy" topic.
  • The Tiered System: Categorize your friends.
  • Tier 1:* People who can handle the raw truth.
  • Tier 2:* People who get the "I'm hanging in there" summary.
  • Tier 3:* People who only get "Fine, thanks!"
  • * Save your vulnerability for Tier 1 to prevent the sting of rejection from Tiers 2 and 3.
  • Direct Communication (High Energy Cost): If the relationship is valuable, you can address the silence.
  • Script:* "I noticed the conversation stalled when I mentioned my health. I don't need you to fix it, I just value our connection and wanted to be honest."

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do people ask 'how are you' if they don't want the truth? Most people operate on social autopilot. They are using a polite greeting ritual and are not consciously prepared for a heavy or complex answer. It is rarely a calculated trap, but rather a lack of social awareness regarding chronic illness.

Should I just lie and say I'm fine? That is a personal choice. Masking (pretending to be well) takes energy, but facing rejection also takes energy. You have to weigh which cost is higher for you on any given day. Many find a middle ground—a brief, vague truth—is the most sustainable.

Does this mean my friends don't care about me? Not necessarily. It often means they care but feel paralyzed by their inability to help. However, consistent ghosting suggests a mismatch in emotional needs. It is painful, but accepting their limitations can free you from the cycle of expectation and disappointment.

How do I make the rejection hurt less? Shift your focus from "seeking validation" to "observing behavior." When someone doesn't reply, try to view it as data about their capacity, rather than a judgment on your life. Remind yourself: "This is about their discomfort with illness, not my lovability."

Key Takeaways

  • The Non-Response is common: You are not alone; this is a universal experience in the chronic illness community.
  • It’s not your fault: Being honest about your reality is not a social crime. The silence is usually due to the listener's lack of emotional tools.
  • Protect your energy: If a specific person consistently ghosts you after health updates, consider lowering your vulnerability with them to protect yourself from hurt.
  • Validate yourself: You do not need a text back to confirm that your struggle is real and that you are doing your best.
